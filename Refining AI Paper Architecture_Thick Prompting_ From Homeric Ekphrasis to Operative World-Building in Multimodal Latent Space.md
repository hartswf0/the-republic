# **Thick Prompting: From Homeric Ekphrasis to Operative World-Building in Multimodal Latent Space**

## **1\. Introduction: The Crisis of Fidelity in Generative Media**

The contemporary landscape of generative artificial intelligence is currently paralyzed by a crisis of evaluation, a methodological impasse where the metrics of success—fidelity, photorealism, and statistical alignment—have severed the medium from its creative potential. For the better part of the last decade, the dominant paradigm for evaluating multimodal models has been representational correctness. In the engineering literature, systems are benchmarked by their ability to produce outputs that are indistinguishable from "ground truth" data, minimizing Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) scores and maximizing the statistical overlap between training and generation distributions.1 This obsession with mimesis, while technically necessary for the maturation of diffusion models and transformers, has produced a flattened aesthetic culture where the tool is viewed merely as an engine of reproduction rather than an instrument of *poiesis*.

This report argues that such metrics fail to capture the expressive potential of multimodal models. When a user engages with a high-dimensional latent space—whether through Stable Diffusion, Midjourney, or Large Language Models (LLMs)—they are not simply retrieving a file or requesting a copy. They are engaging in a complex act of linguistic and semiotic transmutation where the signifier (the prompt) forces the signified (the image, the concept) into material existence.2 The current user interface paradigm, characterized by "thin prompting"—functional, distinct commands aimed at simple retrieval like "show me a cat"—is insufficient for navigating the complex interiority of modern AI. It treats the model as a search engine rather than a generative cosmology.

To transcend this limitation, we propose **Thick Prompting**, a method that treats prompts not as simple instructions but as "heterogeneous stacks" executed as a Geertzian ritual to navigate high-dimensional latent space. This methodology reconceptualizes the prompt as a dense, multilayered artifact combining natural language, code, aesthetic tags, and parametric constraints. However, to understand the future of this interaction, we must look backward. The dream of "words creating worlds" is not a novelty of Silicon Valley but the oldest ambition of Western literature. By grounding our analysis in the classical tradition of Homer’s *Shield of Achilles*, we uncover the proto-algorithmic logic of ekphrasis—the verbal representation of visual representation.

The following analysis traces the genealogy of the prompt from the forges of the *Iliad* to the GPU clusters of the 21st century. We argue that Homer did not merely describe a shield; he narrated the *process* of its forging, embedding time, motion, and sound into static metal, providing the necessary blueprint for understanding generative AI as a forge for "operative world-building".4 We then overlay this with Clifford Geertz’s anthropological distinction between a "thin" physical contraction (a twitch) and a "thick" social gesture (a wink), arguing that effective interaction with latent space requires a similar density of cultural coding.5 Finally, we bridge these theories using Hannes Bajohr’s concept of "operative ekphrasis," demonstrating how description becomes an active, generative force in the age of artificial intelligence.2

## **2\. The Homeric Anchor: The Shield of Achilles as Theoretical Blueprint**

To theorize the prompt, we must first theorize the relationship between word and image in its most primordial form. The canonical example of this relationship in the Western canon is the *Shield of Achilles*, described in Book 18 of the *Iliad*.4 This passage is widely recognized as the first instance of **ekphrasis**—the dramatic, verbal description of a visual work of art. However, a close reading reveals that Homer’s description is not static; it is procedural, algorithmic, and generative. It serves as the *ur-text* for the text-to-image workflow.

### **2.1 The Shield as Process: The First Generative Script**

When Thetis asks Hephaestus to forge armor for her son Achilles, the lame god does not simply hand her a finished product. Homer takes the audience into the forge, describing the *making* of the object. Hephaestus throws copper, tin, silver, and gold into the fire, and the text follows the sequence of creation.8 This distinction is crucial: the *Shield* is not a snapshot of a world; it is a simulation of one.

Scholars such as Becker (1990) and Taplin (1980) have long noted that the scenes depicted on the shield—the city at peace and the city at war, the ploughmen in the fields, the dancing youths—are described with a liveliness that defies the medium of metal.4 The figures move, the earth turns black behind the plough "though it was gold," and the sound of the *linos* song rises from the vineyard. The ekphrasis presents a "process of depicting human life—not an image of the world but a negotiation with it".4

In the context of generative AI, Hephaestus functions as the generative model (the neural network), and Homer’s text serves as the prompt. The text does not merely label objects ("a shield," "a city"); it specifies the *parameters of their simulation*. The poet describes the intent, the materials, and the motion, and the "god" executes the rendering. This foreshadows the mechanic of modern diffusion models, where the user does not paint the pixels but defines the conditions under which the pixels must coalesce. The *Shield* represents a shift from *mimesis* (imitation) to *poiesis* (making), where the description triggers the creation of the object described.

### **2.2 Metalepsis and the Breach of the Frame**

A key feature of the Homeric shield is **metalepsis**, a narratological trope where the boundaries between distinct narrative levels are breached. On the shield, the boundaries between the "real" world of the poem and the "depicted" world of the art object dissolve. The figures on the shield are said to "speak" and "hear," and the narrator interprets their inner thoughts, noting that women "marveled" (*thaumazon*) at the wedding procession.9

This is analogous to the phenomenon of "hallucination" or "emergence" in Large Language Models and image generators. When a model generates a detail that was not explicitly requested but is contextually consistent—a "thick" detail—it is performing a kind of algorithmic metalepsis. It is pulling from the "Worldtext" (the training data) to fill in the gaps of the user's prompt.10 The shield is organized by concentric circles, moving from the cosmos (sun, moon, stars) in the center, through human civilization (cities), to the rural outskirts, and finally bounded by the River Oceanus.9 This is a cosmological map, a "microcosm of the universe".9 Similarly, the latent space of a model like Stable Diffusion is a high-dimensional cosmology, a mathematical map of all potential concepts and their relations. To prompt is to traverse this map, to draw a circle around a specific coordinate and say, "Let there be a city here."

### **2.3 Denotation vs. Depiction: The Logic of the Prompt**

Crucially, the Homeric ekphrasis is not concerned with "photorealism" in the modern sense, but with **denotation**. It intends to "direct and channel attention," making the intelligible visible.4 The prompt "a city at peace" calls forth a specific set of cultural associations: weddings, law courts, festivals. The prompt "a city at war" calls forth sieges, ambushes, and strife.

This distinction is vital for "Thick Prompting." A "thin" prompt asks for a visual match ("a picture of a shield"). A "thick" prompt, like Homer’s, asks for the *relational dynamics* of the object ("a shield depicting two cities, one where a wedding song arises, another where an ambush is laid"). The former produces a static texture; the latter produces a narrative world. The "marvel" (*thauma*) of the shield lies not in its accuracy, but in its ability to contain a dynamic world within a static frame.4 Generative AI, when used to its full potential, aims for this same *thauma*: the compression of a vast, dynamic narrative space into a generated artifact.

## **3\. The Semiotic Web: Geertzian Thick Description in Latent Space**

If Homer provides the poetic ancestor of the prompt, the anthropologist Clifford Geertz provides its semiotic operating system. In his seminal essay "Thick Description: Toward an Interpretive Theory of Culture" (1973), Geertz argues that culture is a "web of significance" that man himself has spun.6 To understand culture is not to catalogue laws or behaviors, but to interpret the meaning of symbols within that web. This anthropological framework is the essential key to unlocking the "black box" of the neural network.

### **3.1 The Wink and the Twitch: Anatomy of a Prompt**

Geertz, borrowing from philosopher Gilbert Ryle, illustrates the concept of "thick description" with the famous example of the wink. Consider two boys: one has an involuntary eyelid twitch; the other winks at a friend to signal a conspiracy. Photographically—or in terms of raw pixel data—the two events are identical. A "thin description" of both would be: "rapid contraction of the right eyelid".6

However, the wink differs radically from the twitch because it is deeply embedded in a social code. It is deliberate, directed at someone, intended to impart a message, executed according to a socially established code, and done without the cognizance of the rest of the company.6 A "thick description" captures this hierarchy of meaningful structures. It distinguishes the wink from the twitch, and further distinguishes the *parody* of a wink, or the *rehearsal* of a parody of a wink.

In the context of generative AI, the distinction between a "thin" and "thick" prompt is identical.

* **Thin Prompt:** "An astronaut riding a horse." This is the "twitch." It asks for the raw visual signifier, the contraction of the eyelid. The model will produce the object, but it will be semantically hollow, a generic average of all astronauts and all horses in the dataset.  
* **Thick Prompt:** "A grainy, overexposed photograph of an astronaut riding a weary horse through a dust storm in the style of 1960s Soviet propaganda, evoking a sense of exhausted futility." This is the "wink." It embeds the object in a specific web of significance (Cold War aesthetics, emotional exhaustion, analog photography artifacts). It relies on "socially established codes" (the visual language of propaganda, the semiotics of "grain") that the model has learned during training.

### **3.2 Latent Space as a Cultural Archive**

The "Latent Space" of a machine learning model is, effectively, a mathematical encoding of Geertz’s "web of significance." During training, the model ingests billions of image-text pairs, learning not just what objects look like, but how they relate to one another in the vast archive of human culture.13 It learns that "wedding" correlates with "white dress," "joy," "flowers," and "guests," just as Homer associated the city at peace with "torches blazing" and "hymenal song".9

When a user prompts a model, they are performing an act of **ethnography** on this artificial culture. They are trying to "read" the latent space, to find the specific cluster of variables that corresponds to their intent. A "thick prompt" is a mechanism for triangulation; it adds enough semantic constraints to navigate away from the generic mean (the "thin" description) and toward a specific, culturally loaded region of the latent space.5 As Geertz notes, "Analysis, then, is sorting out the structures of signification".15 "Thick Prompting" is the sorting out of the model's learned structures. It requires the prompter to understand the "codes" the model speaks—not just English syntax, but the "artificial semantics" of tags, styles, and aesthetic markers.7

### **3.3 The Interpretation of Cultures vs. The Generation of Cultures**

Geertzian anthropology is interpretive; it looks at existing cultures and tries to explain them. Generative AI is, by definition, projective; it uses cultural data to synthesize *new* artifacts. However, the mechanism is the same. To generate a convincing image of a fictional culture (e.g., "a cyberpunk street market in Neo-Tokyo"), the prompter must employ thick description to establish the "normalness" of that world without reducing its particularity.15

If the prompt is too thin ("cyberpunk city"), the model defaults to clichés (neon lights, rain). If the prompt is "thick" (specifying the socio-economic decay, the specific mix of analog and digital technology, the architectural influences), the model can synthesize a "culture" that feels lived-in and coherent. The prompter must perform the "intellectual effort" of specifying the *context*—the "stratified hierarchy of meaningful structures" in which the image exists.12 The user must engage in "theory sensitive" interpretive work, recognizing that the model's output is an "inductive conceptual leap" based on the input data.5

## **4\. The Hinge: From Theory to Practice (The "Operative" Bridge)**

If Sections 1 through 3 have served to name the historical and anthropological lineage of the prompt—grounding it in the *Shield of Achilles* and the Geertzian wink—Sections 5 and 6 demonstrate what these theories *do* when operationalized in the software environment. We have established that a prompt is a "thick" cultural act. But in a computational system, culture must become code. The hinge between these two realities is the concept of the **operative**.

Hannes Bajohr (2024) provides the critical intervention here. He argues that in the transition from analog to digital, and specifically to machine learning, we witness the birth of "**operative ekphrasis**".2 In the classical tradition, ekphrasis was a representational device: words describing an image that exists elsewhere (or nowhere). In the digital realm, however, the text does not merely describe the image; it *executes* it. As Bajohr notes, "Multimodal AI... collapses the distinction between text and image by circumventing this separation altogether".16 The text is the causal agent; the image is the "effect of the textual prompt".2

This shift from representation to operation transforms the "thick description" from an interpretive essay into a generative script. A "thick prompt" is not just a detailed description; it is a set of operative instructions that manipulate the mathematical weights of the neural network. It is a "Geertzian ritual" that functions as a command line. Bajohr suggests that this creates a form of "artificial semantics" where the "representation spaces of text and images become fused".13 The "world-building" of Homer is no longer a metaphor for the poet's imagination but a literal description of the model's computational process. **Thick Prompting**, therefore, is the practical method of navigating this collapsed space, treating the prompt as a heterogeneous stack of cultural symbols and operative code. It is the practical method by which the user asserts agency over the "artificial semantics" of the machine.

## **5\. Defining "Thick Prompting": The Heterogeneous Stack**

Having established the theoretical "what" and "why," we now turn to the "how." **Thick Prompting** is the methodological application of operative ekphrasis. It rejects the notion of the prompt as a simple natural language sentence (e.g., "draw a cat"). Instead, it conceptualizes the prompt as a **Heterogeneous Stack**—a composite structure that amalgamates distinct types of linguistic and code-based inputs to triangulate a location in latent space.

### **5.1 The Components of the Stack**

A "Thick Prompt" is rarely a single coherent sentence. It is a collage of operative layers, each serving a distinct function in the navigation of the model's internal topography. We can identify four primary layers in this stack, which blend code, text, tags, and structured data formats (like JSON) to achieve precision.

#### **5.1.1 The Core Ekphrasis (Natural Language)**

This is the Homeric layer. It describes the subject and action using natural language. However, unlike human-to-human communication, this layer must be "thickened" with semantic triggers that the model recognizes. This layer sets the "attention" mechanism of the Transformer architecture, defining the primary tokens that the model must attend to.1

* *Example:* Instead of "a robot," the core might read "A rusted, diesel-punk automaton, leaking oil, kneeling in prayer."  
* *Function:* Defines the primary semantic content and the relations between objects. It creates the "scene" in the Homeric sense.

#### **5.1.2 The Stylistic/Cultural Tags (The Geertzian Codes)**

This layer consists of keywords, tags, and references that tap into specific clusters of the training data. These are often comma-separated lists that function as "winks" to the model, signaling the aesthetic regime. These tags are "operative" because they shift the vector path of the generation toward specific regions of the high-dimensional space known to contain these styles.13

* *Example:* "Kodachrome, 8mm film grain, Tarkovsky-esque, chiaroscuro, volumetric lighting, brutalist architecture, trending on ArtStation."  
* *Function:* These tokens do not describe *objects* but *modes of seeing*. They orient the model toward specific "vibes" or aesthetic lineages. They effectively "call forth" the style by invoking the cultural archive.

#### **5.1.3 The Parametric Constraints (The Code)**

These are the non-linguistic controls that govern the generation process. In systems like Midjourney or Stable Diffusion, these include aspect ratios (--ar 16:9), chaos values (--c 50), stylized values (--s 750), and seed numbers (--seed 12345).17

* *Function:* These are purely operative. They have no semantic meaning in the Geertzian sense (a "--ar 16:9" has no cultural "wink" inherent to it, though it evokes the cinema screen), but they constrain the "world" of the image physically. They are the "physics engine" of the prompt.

#### **5.1.4 The Negative Prompt (The Apophatic Layer)**

Crucially, Thick Prompting involves defining what the world is *not*. The "Negative Prompt" allows the user to subtract concepts from the generation (e.g., "no text, no blur, no cartoon, no deformation, no low quality").18

* *Function:* This is a form of *apophatic* world-building (definition by negation). It carves out the negative space, ensuring the "thick description" is not contaminated by the "thin" averages of the dataset (e.g., the tendency of AI to default to "cartoonish" styles or "bad anatomy").

#### **5.1.5 Structured Data (JSON/Weights)**

Advanced Thick Prompting often incorporates structured data formats like JSON or weighted tokens (e.g., (fog:1.5), \[blue:red:0.5\]). This allows for precise control over the influence of specific concepts, akin to weighting the materials in Hephaestus's forge.

* *Function:* Provides algorithmic precision to the semantic weight of terms, allowing the user to fine-tune the "thickness" of specific descriptions.

### **5.2 Prompt Engineering as Ritual Performance**

The execution of this heterogeneous stack is not a linear "input-output" transaction. It is a recursive, ritualistic process. The prompt engineer does not "write" the image; they "summon" it through iteration. Researchers and practitioners have explicitly compared this process to "shamanic ritual" or "occult summoning".20

This comparison is not merely colorful metaphor; it reflects the **probabilistic** nature of the interaction. The prompt engineer is negotiating with an opaque entity (the "black box" of the neural network). They offer a "spell" (the prompt), observe the manifestation (the image), and then refine the incantation.

* *The "Micro-Ritual":* As described in practitioner guides, this involves running A/B tests, locking seed numbers (the "chaos" variable), and iteratively tweaking single words.17  
* *The "Golden Set":* The maintenance of a "grimoire" of effective prompts—phrases that are known to reliably "summon" high-quality results across different models. "Golden Set" tasks are used to benchmark new prompts against known successes.17

This ritualistic aspect brings us back to Geertz. The "thick prompt" is a performance. The user is not just operating a tool; they are performing a role (often explicitly, by telling the AI "You are an expert photographer..." or "Act as a 19th-century poet...").17 This role-playing induces the model to adopt a specific "persona" from its training data, effectively narrowing the search space to the desired cultural context.

## **6\. Case Studies: The Operative Ekphrasis of Meerhoff and Orr**

To illustrate the practical difference between "classic" digital generation and the "thick" operative ekphrasis we are proposing, we turn to the comparison provided by Bajohr (2024) between the works of Jasmin Meerhoff and Dave Orr. This comparison highlights the shift from code-driven text to prompt-driven hallucination.7

### **6.1 Jasmin Meerhoff: The Explicit Code**

Jasmin Meerhoff’s work, such as *They Lay* (2022), represents the "sequential" paradigm of digital text. Her visual poetry is generated by explicit code (shell scripts) that manipulates text files.7

* *Mechanism:* Code ![][image1] Execution ![][image1] Visual Output.  
* *Nature:* The relationship is causal and mechanical. The code "writes" the text. There is a clear distinction between the tool and the product. The code is the "score," and the output is the "performance." This aligns with the "first text" (code) and "second text" (output) duality observed by Hayles and Aarseth.7

### **6.2 Dave Orr: The Hallucinated Text**

Dave Orr’s piece, *Stiny Snity Grify* (2022), represents a radical departure. It was generated using DALL·E 2 with the prompt "a poem about the singularity written in a serif font".7

* *Mechanism:* Natural Language ![][image1] Latent Space ![][image1] Visual Hallucination of Text.  
* *Nature:* Here, the distinction collapses. The AI generated an image *of* text. The text in the image ("Stiny Snity Grify") is semantic nonsense ("dumb meaning"), yet it *looks* like a poem.23

Orr’s work demonstrates **Operative Ekphrasis** in its purest form. The prompt did not script the pixels (as Meerhoff’s code did); it "called forth" the *concept* of a poem from the latent space. The model accessed the "vibe" of poetry—stanzas, serif fonts, paper texture—and rendered it. The resulting text is asemic (without semantic content), but the *image* is fully thick with the *significance* of poetry. It is a "wink" without the eye; a "thick description" of a poem that does not exist.

This phenomenon forces us to confront the nature of "meaning" in AI. As Bajohr notes, Orr's poem "means what it shows" on a technical, non-intentional level.7 It encodes the *description* of itself within itself. The "thick prompt" here did not specify the letters; it specified the *genre*. The model filled in the "thick" details (the layout, the font, the wear on the paper) based on its training on millions of images of poems. This is Geertzian "cultural text" operationalized: the model knows what a poem *is* as a cultural artifact, even if it cannot read or write one in the human sense.

## **7\. Artificial Semantics and the "Worldtext"**

The phenomenon of Orr’s "Stiny Snity Grify" leads us to a deeper theoretical problem: if the AI generates a "poem" that means nothing, where does the meaning reside? This brings us to the concept of **Artificial Semantics** and the **Worldtext**, borrowing terminology from both Buddhist Tantra and Digital Humanities to describe the totality of the training corpus.10

### **7.1 Dumb Meaning vs. Artificial Semantics**

Bajohr distinguishes between "dumb meaning" (the output of a stochastic parrot, mere statistical correlation) and "artificial semantics".23

* **Dumb Meaning:** The AI has no intent. It does not "know" what a poem is. It only knows that the token "poem" is statistically associated with the visual patterns of stanzas and serif fonts.  
* **Artificial Semantics:** However, the system exhibits a "primitive form of artificial semantics" because the *textual* prompt effectively manipulates the *visual* output.7 The meaning is not in the mind of the machine, but in the **operative connection** between the word and the image.

When we use Thick Prompting, we are leveraging this artificial semantics. We are treating the model *as if* it understands culture. When we add the tag "brutalist architecture," we are not teaching the model architectural history; we are activating the high-dimensional vector cluster where "concrete," "monumental," and "geometric" intersect. The "meaning" is purely **topological**—it is a location in the map.

### **7.2 The Worldtext**

This topological map constitutes what some scholars call the **Worldtext**.10 In Buddhist Tantra, the "letters of the worldtext" are the fundamental constituents of reality, dependent and co-originating. In the Digital Humanities, we can appropriate this term to describe the **training corpus**.

The Worldtext is the aggregate of all digitized human culture—the billions of webpages, books, and images scraped to train the model. This is the "sedimented history" of human expression. The latent space is the compressed holographic representation of this Worldtext.

* **The Prompt as Navigation:** Thick Prompting is the art of navigating the Worldtext. A "thin" prompt stays on the surface, retrieving the most common, average representation (the "cliché"). A "thick" prompt dives deep into the specific strata of the Worldtext, retrieving the esoteric, the specific, and the nuanced.  
* **The "Vibe" as Ontology:** In this space, the "vibe" becomes a rigorous ontological category.13 A "vibe" is essentially a coherent region of latent space—a cluster of style, mood, and subject matter that hangs together statistically. To prompt for a "vibe" (e.g., "vaporwave" or "film noir") is to activate a specific region of the Worldtext.

### **7.3 Operative World-Building**

We return, finally, to the *Shield of Achilles*. Homer’s genius was to realize that to describe the world, one must describe the *forces* that shape it (the ploughing, the dancing, the judging).

**Thick Prompting** applies this realization to AI. It moves beyond "noun-based" prompting ("an astronaut") to "verb/adjective/context-based" prompting ("an astronaut *struggling* against the wind, *bathed* in harsh red light, *rendered* in the impasto style").

This is **Operative World-Building**. The prompter is not asking for a picture; they are setting the initial conditions for a simulation. They are defining the physics (light, texture), the history (style, era), and the narrative (action, emotion) of a micro-world. The AI then "runs" this simulation to produce the final frame. As Bajohr notes, in this paradigm, "Il n'y a rien hors du modèle"—there is nothing outside the model.26 The model contains the potential for all worlds; the prompt is the key that unlocks one specific iteration.

## **8\. Conclusion: The Return to Poiesis**

The critique of generative AI as "derivative" or "soulless" often stems from an analysis of "thin" interactions—users entering simple commands and receiving generic, averaged outputs. This report has argued that such a view ignores the operative potential of the medium. By adopting the framework of **Thick Prompting**, we reveal the "heterogeneous stack" of the prompt to be a sophisticated instrument of **poiesis** (making/creation).

We have traced a direct lineage from the Homeric forge to the GPU cluster:

1. **Homer** taught us that description is a process of dynamic world-building, not static mirroring. The ekphrasis of the shield provides the first model of a "thick" generative script.  
2. **Geertz** taught us that meaning resides in the "thick" web of cultural codes, not in the "thin" physical fact. To engage with the "artificial culture" of latent space, we must use "thick" descriptions that leverage these codes.  
3. **Bajohr** taught us that in the digital age, description becomes **operative**—language acts directly upon the substrate of the image. The "hinge" of the operative transforms the prompt from a caption into a command.

The "Thick Prompt" is the realization of Operative Ekphrasis. It is a Geertzian ritual executed in a Homeric mode. It requires the user to be more than a consumer of content; they must become an ethnographer of the latent space and an architect of the Worldtext.

In this light, the "fidelity" of an AI image matters less than its **density**—the richness of the cultural and semantic web it successfully activates. The future of generative AI lies not in better simulating reality, but in better navigating the "possibility space" of the imagination. As with the Shield of Achilles, the goal is to create an artifact that contains a world—a "marvel" that compels the viewer to wonder not just at *what* is depicted, but *how* it came to be. The prompt engineer, like Hephaestus, stands at the forge, throwing language into the fire to see what worlds might rise from the flames.
